Geography, Climate & Biodiversity

Overview

The Cape York Peninsula is a true peninsula, extending 800km from the Australian mainland into the Torres Strait, surrounded by water on most of its borders and connected to the mainland at the northeastern coast of Australia, stretching 650km wide. Large, meandering river systems flow west from the peninsula ridge of the Great Dividing Range (large mountain range), into Gulf of Carpentaria, a large shallow sea enclosed on three sides by northern Australia. To the east, short fast flowing rivers (lined with dense rainforests, sand dunes and mangroves) provide freshwater and important nutrients to the biodiverse rich Coral Sea. Coral reefs surround the peninsula and are home to a rich diversity of life. Most notably the world’s largest and most extensive coral reef ecosystem, the Great Barrier Reef. It also holds great scientific interest as the habitat of species such as the dugong (“sea cow”) and the large green turtle, which are threatened with extinction. Since 1995 half the corals on the Great Barrier Reef, mostly around Cape York, have died due to climate change induced coral bleaching. The climate of Cape York is tropical and humid, with high average temperatures (27oC-31oC) and high rainfall. The northern tip of the peninsula and coastal areas receives approximately 2,000mm or more of rainfall per year, while the southern inland regions of the peninsula are drier and receive around 700mm or less of rain.

The monsoon season generally occurs between November and April, bringing to life vast areas of aquatic swamplands and flash flooding. Areas become difficult to navigate and inhabit, as rivers swell and spread across extensive floodplains. Humidity drops and temperatures rise, increasing the risk of bushfires across the peninsula. The Cape York Peninsula supports a complex ecosystem that ranges from tropical rainforests, savannahs and grasslands to coastal vegetation, mangroves and wetlands. National parks that are scattered along the central and eastern regions of the peninsula, protect a significantly biodiverse landscape. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders significantly shaped and transformed the landscape through sustainable land management practices over tens of thousands of years. In contrast, European colonisation caused extensive change by clearing native vegetation and forests, damming rivers, building urban centres and establishing intensive and increasingly industrialised agricultural systems over 250 years. Vast areas of land have also been degraded from pastoral activity and mining, before being handed back to the Traditional Owners.
