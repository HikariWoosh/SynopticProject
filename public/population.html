In 1788 the Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander population was officially estimated to be around 770,000. However, this figure is based on limited, unreliable and highly racially biased records and impressions of early colonists, and may have actually been in the millions. Today the Australian government works closely with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities to ensure people can be counted in census data. The 2016 Census of Population and Housing, and National Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Social Survey 2014/15 provides population and demographic data on the Indigenous Regions of the Cape York Peninsula. However, due to the tools and processes associated with data gathering, cultural bias, inaccurate information, regular movement and non-nuclear family structures, the census data can potentially and significantly underestimate the true remote Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander population figures.
Areas in Australia are classified as “Very remote Australia”, “Remote Australia”, “Outer regional Australia”, “Inner regional Australia” and “Major cities of Australia”. Approximately 30% of Queensland’s total Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander population were living in major cities, 51% in regional areas, 7% in remote areas and 12% in very remote areas. In remote areas, communities are often described in terms of population size and determined by road distance from urban centres, rather than geographical area or the number of dwellings. For example:
• Homelands/Outstations - An Indigenous settlement that relies on another community for services. Generally 50 people or fewer and can often consist of one to ten houses. The term homeland and outstation can be used interchangeably.
• Discrete Communities - Large Indigenous communities. Discrete communities are remote, supplied by government utilities and typically operate as service areas for surrounding outstations. A major community is generally around 200 to 1000 people while a minor community is generally fewer than 200 people.
