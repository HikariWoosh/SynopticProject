A Brief History of Cape York

-

More Than 65,000 Years Ago

Artefacts found at Madjedbeb, Mirrarr Country in Northern Arnhem Land date back more than 65,000 years old. The foundation of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander spiritual beliefs and cultural history goes back beyond concepts of time to an era known as the “Dreaming”

30,000 Years Ago

Fragments of grinding stones found at the archaeological site at Cuddie Springs in New South Wales.

20,000 Years Ago

Aboriginal communities are established throughout coastal and mainland Australia and Tasmania

8000-12000 Years Ago

Present day Australian climate is established and seas rise from an ancient coast line at the very outer reaches of the Great Barrier Reef. Some traditional stories, songs and dances tell of this time as the waters rushed into the present day coast.

2500 Years Ago

The first Torres Strait Islanders settled in the archipelago from what is now Papua New Guinea and from the Pacific Islands of Melanesia. They are ethnically and culturally distinct from Aboriginal Australians

1500

Dutch documents recorded the journey of Maccassan trepangers to northern Australia.

1770

Captain James Cook lands in Botany Bay (present day Sydney) and claimed Australia as a territory of Great Britain, despite the land being inhabited for tens of thousands of years

1788-1880

“Frontier Wars” begin, these include all the massacres, wars and instances of resistance beginning from the arrival of colonists in 1788. Approximately 90% of the Aboriginal population lost their lives due to the conflicts. Gold is discovered and mining for other minerals begins.

1897

The “Aboriginal Protection and Restriction of the Sale of Opium Act 1897” was introduced, controlling and oppressing Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders for most of the 20th century. The act was further strengthened by repeated amendments

1901

The Constitution of Australia is written that establishes Australia as a Federation. The Commonwealth of Australia is formed without any consultation with Australia’s First Nation

1910-1970

Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander children were forcibly taken from their families and communities as a result of colonisation, regulations, and laws. Referred to as the “Stolen Generation”. The pain of the stolen generations still resonates heavily in communities. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders serve on land, air and sea during World War One and World War Two. One in 20 contributed to Australia’s World War Two efforts, as servicemen or women, or civilian labourers. Australia suffers economic impacts during the Great Depression in the 1930s. Australia claims 42% of Antarctica in 1935

1962

The mining town of Weipa on the west coast was built by the Rio Tinto Aluminium company on the west coast of Cape York to ship bauxite to Japan. It is the largest town in Cape York and the third largest bauxite mine in the world.

1963

The first formal attempt of Indigenous land claims by the Yolgnu people after discovering a vast mining lease on traditional land failed

1966

Senior Elder Vincent Lingiari led two hundred of his people, employees of Wave Hill station, with their families, in a walk-off, demanding better pay and rations, and protection of the Aboriginal women. This action galvanised national debate on Indigenous rights

1967

A national referendum removed references from the Australian constitution which discriminated against Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples from being counted in the national census with the highest ever “yes” vote in an Australian referendum (over 90%) The world’s largest silica sand mine is built on the eastern coast of the Cape York Peninsula in Cape Flattery

1975

The Australian Parliament passes the Racial Discrimination Act to help ensure that Australians of all backgrounds are treated equally and receive the same opportunities

1976

The Australian Government started purchasing privately owned land for the benefit of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander communities to be made available for claim under the Aboriginal Land Rights (Northern Territory) Act 1976

1992

The “Mabo Case”, recognised in Australian law for the first time the rights of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples to their lands based on their traditional connection to and occupation of their Country.

1993

Ownership of the Starcke Station is returned to the Traditional Owners of the land. Setting the groundwork for the Cape York Tenure Resolution Programme. The Native Title Act 1993 is introduced which recognises Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples rights and interests for land and water as compliant with their laws and cultures. National Reconciliation Week occurs between 27 May to 3 June, to commemorate the successful 1967 referendum, and the High Court Mabo decision.

1990s-2000s

The processes recognising Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders as Traditional Owners and returning land rights across Cape York moved forward via legislation such as the Aboriginal Land Act 1991 and the Cape York Peninsula Heritage Act 2007.

1996

The Council for Aboriginal Reconciliation launches Australia’s first National Reconciliation Week

1997

The “Bringing Them Home” report on Australia’s Stolen Generations was launched at the inaugural Australian Reconciliation Convention in May 1997. National “Sorry Day” is acknowledged annually on the 26 May on the anniversary of the report.

2001

Reconciliation Australia is established

2008

A motion is passed to declare a national apology for the mistreatment and injustice to Australia’s Indigenous population.

2009

Australia supports the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous People.

2013

The Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Peoples Recognition Bill 2012 recognises the unique and special place of First Nations people as the original owners of the Australian continent and islands

2014

The Australian Government approved the construction of Carmichael coal mine in Central Queensland, providing the company, Adani, freehold title to the land despite the objections of the Wangan and Jagalingou people, the native title holders.

2017

The National Constitutional Convention at Uluru releases the “Uluru Statement from the Heart” calling for major changes to the Australian Constitution, including a “First Nations Voice” to Parliament, which was rejected

2018-2019

Victoria, Queensland and the Northern Territory establish their own processes towards treaties with Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander nations.

2020

Efforts and funding provided for local Indigenous controlled health agencies to run a culturally aware response and support Indigenous businesses to protect Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders from COVID-19.
