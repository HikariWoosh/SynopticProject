Culture of Cape York

Introduction to Culture

Culture is an umbrella term regarded as the set of distinctive spiritual, material, intellectual and emotional features of society or a social group. It also encompasses art and literature, lifestyles, ways of living together, value systems, traditions and beliefs. Cultural diversity is embodied in the uniqueness and identities of the groups and societies making up humankind. As a source of exchange, innovation and creativity, cultural diversity is as necessary for humankind as biodiversity is for nature. Cultural pluralism is the respect for the diversity of people, where minority groups can participate fully in society and maintain their cultural identity. In all we do, we should be enabling inclusion, equity and participation of all citizens, which aids social cohesion, the vitality of civil society and peace. Culture in relation to sustainable development plays an important role not only in promoting, but also enabling sustainable development. Together with economic prosperity, social justice and environmental sustainability, culture is often recognised as the fourth pillar of sustainable development. It may be interesting to note that there is also culture developed within companies and universities, which is why it is so important to value and include various perspectives that widen our understanding and challenge our assumptions, approaches, values and beliefs. This will determine how we act as future professionals within engineering, ensuring we deliver on our responsibility to include cultural diversity in how we approach solution creation

Overview

Australia has always been multicultural. Before colonisation there were over 250 language groups including over 800 dialects, and many diverse cultures across the continent. Communities were connected through highly organised trading partnerships in complex intertribal and socio-cultural relationships for tens of thousands of years. Aboriginal Australians and Torres Strait Islanders have distinct and diverse cultural histories, and maintain strong connections between each other in Cape York. Spiritual connection and belonging to Country is central to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander culture, creating a deep connection with the land and the sea. It is sustained by a range of human practices, including effective resource use such as traditional fire management. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples don’t generally see themselves as owning or possessing the land but rather as caretakers of the land. As an oral culture (i.e. spoken from one generation to the next), a deep and complex educational system was embedded in the process of passing on knowledge and wisdom through the landscape, through stories, ritual and rites. “Songlines”, that can cross thousands of kilometers of the continent, require maintenance through ritual ceremonies and customary songs between many tribal groups. Complex protocols existed with respect to when, where and to whom knowledge could be shared and passed on. Contrary to western cultures views on freedom of information and knowledge systems, Australia’s First Nation communities generally view the passing on of knowledge as a sacred right and something earned through demonstrated wisdom and responsibility.

Terminology and Language

“Reconciliation” means acknowledging Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders as the First People of Australia and recognising the injustices from colonisation. As part of reconciliation, using respectful and inclusive language and terminology is important. Language can impact attitudes, relationships and understandings. The way reconciliation is talked about is just as important as the way people act. We encourage you to consider these guidelines and recommendations when exploring the lives and designing for Aboriginal communities. Using “Aboriginal and/or Torres Strait Islander” is considered best practice. It should always be capitalised and can be considered offensive to abbreviate i.e. ATSI as this is not inclusive of the diversity of cultures and identities across Australia. “Indigenous Australian people” is also commonly used, especially by non-Aboriginal or non-Torres Strait Islander people, however some people find this offensive, as it can be seen as grouping nations together, and prefer the full name. Pluralised terms such as “First Nations”, “First People” or “Aboriginal People” are acceptable language, and respectfully encompass the diversity of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander cultures and identities. When referring to “non-Indigenous people”, terms such as “non-First Nations” or “other Australians” are also acceptable. Avoid using the terms such as “Aborigine”, “disadvantaged”, “native/native Australians” and “lost”, as it is considered offensive as these terms were used historically with derogatory connotations

Acknowledgment of Country

The concept of Country and the term itself is used widely by Indigneous Australians to describe a deep concept embedded in the notion of life and the land and human connection and interconnection to it, with it and in it. Traditional Country is more than solely ownership, the connection to land or a place on a map. It might mean homeland, tribe, clan, values, stories, resources, cultural sites or ancestral domains. When Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people visited the Country of others, there would be rituals of a “Welcome to Country”. As these can only be performed by Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander people, an Acknowledgement of Country is an opportunity for anyone to show respect for and continued connection of Traditional Owners to Country, as recognition to the First Australians and custodians of the land
