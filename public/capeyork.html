Introuction to Cape York

Cape York Peninsula is a large remote peninsula in Queensland, northern Australia, and is the location of this year’s Engineering for People Design Challenge. It is home to Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders who have the longest living continuous cultural history of any group of people on Earth, and are the “Traditional Owners” or “First Nations” people of Australia.

This is your opportunity to explore the remote areas of the Cape York Peninsula, and learn about this amazing culture.

In Cape York, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders are spread across over 100 clan groups and numerous language groups and dialects. Remoteness and large travel distances are common for life on Cape York, with many activities revolving around the wet, monsoonal season and the dry, bushfire season. The region is home to cattle and mining industries, national parks and other protected areas of largely unspoiled wilderness. The term “Country” is often used to describe family origins and associations with particular parts of Australia. Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have cared for land and sea Country as living cultural landscapes across thousands of generations. National parks and other protected areas play a vital role in maintaining and protecting the amazing biodiversity and cultural values.

After the gradual reinstatement of Indigenous land rights in the 1970’s, the “homelands movement” commenced as people voluntarily returned to live back on the land their ancestors occupied prior to colonisation. The revolutionary movement back to homelands is possible through engineering support, enabling the Traditional Owners of the land to live, work and thrive on Country, so they can preserve their culture

Homelands and outstations in Queensland and Cape York are often old, large pastoral farms, called cattle stations, that have been purchased by the Australian Government and handed back with the land. Homelands are also where cultural heritage and knowledge is preserved. Importantly these homelands and outstations manage their own essential services. They receive very little technical and formal assistance from the government and costs for establishing and maintaining infrastructure is high. Specialised skills needed are hard to find and funding and other support is very limited, hard to come by and ad hoc. While they are small and few in number, homelands and outstations are places of strong cultural significance and importance to the Traditional Owners.

Despite the challenges for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islanders returning to their traditional land, homelands and outstations are places where people can develop meaningful livelihoods, exert traditional rights and identity, connect and maintain ancient cultural practices and heal. They are important both physically and symbolically. They represent places that provide access points to Country for a larger clan group and this access facilitates the contemporary practice of culture combined with the management and protection of the land and its rich biodiverse ecosystems, an important part of cultural practice. Living on Homelands provides for a range of improved social and health determinants.

The local Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander controlled response to COVID-19 has generally been viewed as successful, with no deaths and only 149 cases reported across the whole population, as of May 2021. Aboriginal doctors and leaders were acutely aware of how vulnerable communities would be (due to the lack of healthcare in remote areas and Indigenous people having higher risk of complications) to the COVID-19 virus, from lessons learned of the 2009 H1N1 influenza pandemic, and acted quickly. This included public health messaging, flexible grant funding to 110 remote communities and the decision to allow local Indigenous controlled health agencies to run a culturally aware response.

On 18 March 2020, the Government enacted the powers under the Biosecurity Act 2015 to combat the outbreak. This was the first time these powers under the Biosecurity Act have been used, preventing communities from fishing for food, attending school, and leaving to buy essential items like toiletries from nearby larger towns. Across Cape York, roadblocks were set up preventing access to the remote communities. On 12 June 2020, blanket biosecurity laws began to lift in place of rules specific to each region of Australia. While the decision was widely welcomed, Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander leaders in Cape York raised concerns about the pressure from tourism lobby groups to allow travel into vulnerable communities, due to the limited resources available to respond to an outbreak. Since June 2020 travel within Cape York has been open, however some restrictions on entering Queensland reamin in place.
